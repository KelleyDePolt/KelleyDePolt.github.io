Presentations
Statistical Analysis of Gridded Global Climate Datasets Compared to Station Measurements
Kelley De Polt, Heather Aldridge, Aaron Sims
27th Annual NC State University Undergraduate Research & Creativity Spring Symposium, 2018

Global pest forecast models rely on temperature and precipitation data to provide atmospheric conditions at differing resolutions (spatial, temporal). Output from these forecast models assist with protection against the entry, establishment, and further spread of significant pests across the US while also facilitating safe trade processes. This study highlights the accuracy of two global climate datasets, which can be used as inputs into the forecast models within the Spatial Analytical Framework for Advanced Risk Information Systems (SAFARIS) project. Building on previous work that determined the accuracy of NCEP Climate Forecast System Reanalysis (CFSR) and Climate Forecast System Version 2 (CFSv2), the goal of this analysis is to evaluate two additional global climate datasets: ERA-Interim and Climatic Research Unit Time-Series version 4.01. These two datasets are compared with an independent, land-based weather station dataset called International Surface Temperature Initiative’s Global Land Surface Temperature Databank. Quantitative statistics — including the coefficient of determination (R-squared), root mean square error, and bias values — will be presented for maximum and minimum temperature at each grid-point — weather-station pair.

Climate Data Integration for Agricultural Decision Support
Kelley De Polt, Rebecca Cumbie Ward
14th State of North Carolina Undergraduate Research and Creativity Symposium, 2018
American Meteorological Society 99th Meeting, 24th Conference on Applied Climatology, 2019
Project Page

Tobacco thrips, an important vector of Tomato Spotted Wilt Virus (TSWV), feed on crops resulting in stunted growth and delayed maturity along with possible transmission of the virus. These impacts lead to measurable losses that vary depending on time and location, and precautions must be taken before the spread occurs. Researchers at North Carolina State University developed weather-driven models to predict susceptibility of tobacco and cotton, based on the coincidence of plant vulnerability and tobacco thrips incidence. Two decision-support tools have been developed through a partnership with the State Climate Office of North Carolina to provide scientifically-based guidance for management actions to be implemented by growers. The Thrips Infestation Predictor for Cotton along with the Tomato Spotted Wilt Virus and Thrips Risk Forecasting Tool, utilize gridded climate datasets, such as Parameter-Elevation Regressions on Independent Slopes Model climate data, Real-Time Mesoscale MesoScale Analysis products, and National Digital Forecast Database forecast data. Time series of gridded data are obtained based on user-entered location and field conditions, then input into empirical models developed by collaborating researchers within the NCSU Entomology Department. Outputs include predictions for relative thrips flights and TSWV incidence. Users are presented with information contextualized to their locations and historical experiences. A combination of text and interactive graphical model output is displayed to users in a mobile-friendly web interface. These tools, developed through an ongoing, interdisciplinary collaboration, have received widespread praise and have been used by growers and extension agents across North Carolina and the southeastern US during recent planting seasons.
